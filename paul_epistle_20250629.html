Epistle to the Moderns

Paul the Apostle | June 29, 2025

To the saints who gather both physically and virtually, scattered across this digitally woven world, grace and peace be multiplied in you!

It has been a long time since I walked among you in the flesh, yet even in this age of instant communication, I feel a kinship with you, a shared spirit that transcends time and distance. I see your faces flickering on screens, hear your voices echoing in the digital agora, and witness your struggles and triumphs unfold in the newsfeeds that pulse with the lifeblood of your era.

I observe, with both encouragement and concern, the currents of your age. You celebrate unprecedented access to knowledge, yet are drowning in a sea of information, often struggling to discern truth from falsehood. You build bridges of connection across continents with your glowing rectangles, yet feel increasingly isolated and fragmented in your own communities. I see the rise of artificial intelligence, a tool of immense potential, yet one that threatens to blur the lines between human creation and the Divine Creator.

And I see it all reflected in the Saturday morning threads you share, filled with satirical images that, while often humorous, reveal the deep anxieties of your hearts. Political cartoons, though meant to provoke thought, often become echo chambers of division. They point to your exhaustion, your weariness with the constant battles raging in the public square. Even the Church, the very body of Christ, is not immune, fractured by disagreements over doctrine and the interpretation of ancient texts in the face of modern realities.

Let me remind you, then, that these challenges are not new. The world has always been a battleground, a testing ground, a place where the spirit wars with the flesh. The Corinthian church, remember, was rife with division, plagued by moral failings, and entangled in the allure of worldly philosophies. Yet, I urged them, and I urge you now: “Be alert, stand firm in the faith, act like men, be strong. Let all that you do be done in love.” (1 Corinthians 16:13-14)

How do you apply this in your world of algorithms and avatars? It begins, as always, with the Word. But not merely with rote memorization, but with active engagement. When reading the scriptures, consider, as one commenter suggested, the literary style. Pray for discernment, that you may not be swayed by every wind of doctrine, but be firmly rooted in the unchanging truth of the Gospel. Seek wise counsel from those who have walked with Christ for longer than you, not just from the trending voices amplified by social media's algorithms.

And when you find yourself entangled in the threads of online debate, remember the words of Jesus: “Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you…” (Matthew 5:44). This is not weakness, but strength. It is not naive, but divine. To respond to hatred with love, to meet division with unity, to offer grace in the face of judgment – this is how we overcome evil with good.

In a world obsessed with self-promotion and fleeting online validation, the ChurchX concept, though well-intentioned, must be approached with caution. Remember that true ministry is not about building empires or generating revenue, but about serving the least of these. Let not the pursuit of efficiency and accountability eclipse the heart of compassion. Let your love be genuine, not driven by self-interest or the desire for digital applause.

Finally, let the words of Jesus be your compass: “You are the light of the world. A city set on a hill cannot be hidden. Nor do people light a lamp and put it under a basket, but on a stand, and it gives light to all in the house. In the same way, let your light shine before others, so that they may see your good works and give glory to your Father who is in heaven.” (Matthew 5:14-16)

Therefore, go forth, beloved, with courage and compassion. Be bold in your witness, yet gentle in your approach. Let your love for Christ be evident in every tweet, every post, every interaction, both online and in the physical world. Live not in fear of being "left behind," but in joyful anticipation of the day when we shall all be gathered together in His glorious presence.

Now may the God of peace, who brought again from the dead our Lord Jesus, that great shepherd of the sheep, through the blood of the everlasting covenant, make you perfect in every good work to do his will, working in you that which is wellpleasing in his sight, through Jesus Christ; to whom be glory for ever and ever. Amen. (Hebrews 13:20-21)
